# [BRAND] — Merch & Apparel Branding Plan
### Vintage / Heritage Badge system for an offroad/OHV trail-and-park discovery platform

**Aesthetic direction (locked):** Vintage / Heritage Badge — national-park-poster / WPA style, crest & roundel emblems, topographic/contour + compass motifs, earthy vintage palette.
**Audience:** UTV/SxS, ATV, dirt bike, and 4x4/overland riders.
**Strategic goal:** Merch that spreads the name organically. Rigs are mobile billboards; stickers are the community's currency; "I rode this" badges sell on trophy logic. Every physical object should end up on a rig, a jacket, or a cooler — and pull a new rider into the app.

**The one big idea:** Merch is not a revenue line. It is the top of the acquisition funnel and the retention flywheel. The centerpiece is **Earn-a-Badge** (Section 3): riders earn a collectible physical + digital badge for logging a park visit in the app. "Collect all the parks" turns discovery into a game, the game into a habit, and the habit into free advertising stuck to ten thousand tailgates.

## 1. Badge / Identity System

A designer should be able to execute the full system from this section. The north star: it should look like it was printed by the WPA in 1938 and pried off a national-park ranger station, not ordered off Amazon.

### 1.1 Crest / roundel architecture (the emblem family)
Build **one master emblem** with a strict lockup, then derive variants. Consistency of *structure* is what makes a badge system read as heritage rather than clip art.

**Master emblem — the "Trail Seal":**
- **Outer ring (the roundel):** a circular band carrying wordmark text. Top arc = `[BRAND]`. Bottom arc = a rotating tagline slot (`EST. 2026`, `TRAILS & PARKS`, or a park name for commemorative editions). Separate the top and bottom arcs with two small register marks (a compass star, a tire-tread notch, or a 5-point star) at 3 o'clock and 9 o'clock.
- **Inner field (the scene):** a small illustrated landscape — a ridgeline/mountain silhouette, a two-track trail receding to a horizon, sometimes a rising or setting sun with radiating rays (classic WPA sunburst). This is where terrain motifs live.
- **Center anchor:** a single hero motif — a compass rose, a topo contour cluster, or a stylized trail marker.

**Three shapes, one DNA** (a heritage system needs shape variety so it doesn't get monotonous across products):
1. **Roundel** — the circle above. Default for pins, small patches, chest logos, app icon.
2. **Crest / shield** — same scene in a heraldic shield or arrowhead outline. Reads "trophy/achievement" — use this for the Earn-a-Badge program and commemorative editions.
3. **Banner / bar** — a horizontal ribbon or "boxcar" lockup (wordmark over a thin ridgeline rule) for hat fronts, sticker bottoms, and anywhere the round emblem won't fit.

### 1.2 Terrain / topo / compass motif options
Give the designer a motif kit; individual products draw from it so everything feels related without being identical.
- **Topographic contour lines:** thin, evenly-spaced elevation rings used as a background texture, as the center anchor, or ghosted behind the wordmark. The single most "premium outdoor" motif available and it degrades beautifully to one color.
- **Two-track / trail ruts:** two parallel dashed or dotted lines receding to a vanishing point — instantly reads "offroad" without any vehicle, which keeps it multi-discipline (a dirt-bike rider and an overlander both see their sport in a two-track).
- **Compass rose / cardinal star:** 4- or 8-point star, optionally with a fine degree ring. Signals navigation/discovery — on-brand for a *discovery* platform.
- **Ridgeline & sunburst:** layered mountain/mesa silhouettes with a WPA radiating sun. Carries the "national park poster" feel.
- **Pine / sage / desert flora accents:** small botanical marks (pine, sagebrush, saguaro) to regionalize commemorative badges — a Moab badge leans desert/mesa, a Pacific-Northwest badge leans pine/rain.
- **Tire tread as texture:** a subtle tread pattern as a border or fill — use sparingly; it's the one motif that risks tipping into generic "bro" offroad swag if overused.

Rule of thumb: **max two motifs per emblem** (e.g., compass + ridgeline, or topo + two-track). More than that and it turns to mud at sticker scale.

### 1.3 Type-treatment direction
Heritage typography is about pairing a characterful display face with a clean, sturdy workhorse — and setting the roundel text on a curve with generous tracking.
- **Display / wordmark:** a vintage slab serif or a WPA-era condensed gothic. Reference feel: Clarendon, Rockwell, or a heavy geometric sans in the Trade Gothic / Futura Bold family. The wordmark can be custom-lettered for ownership — worth it since it's the single most-reproduced asset.
- **Roundel arc text:** condensed all-caps, wide letter-spacing, small size. This is the "official seal" voice.
- **Secondary / body:** a simple geometric or grotesque sans for taglines, park names, and dates. Keep it quiet so the display face carries the personality.
- **Numerals matter:** commemorative and member badges use big, proud numbers (member #, "EST." year, elevation, trail mileage). Pick a face with characterful numerals.
- **Texture:** allow a light distress/letterpress texture on apparel and posters — but keep a *clean* vector master (no distress) as the source of truth, because stickers and patches must reproduce crisp.

### 1.4 Earthy vintage color palette (name the colors)
A tight, muddy, sun-faded palette. Nothing neon, nothing pure — everything looks like it spent a summer on a dashboard. Give the designer named swatches:

**Core earths (pick 3–4 as primary):**
- **Canvas Cream** `#F2E8CF` — off-white paper base; the "unprinted poster" color.
- **Trail Dust Tan** `#D9C2A3` — warm neutral, secondary backgrounds.
- **Rust Orange / Clay** `#C1502E` — the hero accent; sunset, iron-oxide dirt, energy.
- **Bureau Green / Field Sage** `#4A5D3A` — deep muted olive; the "park service" anchor color.
- **Dusty Denim / Slate Blue** `#3E5A6E` — sky/dusk, cool counterweight to the rust.

**Support / deepening:**
- **Bark Brown** `#5A3E2B` — outlines, wood, near-black for one-color work.
- **Ember Gold / Wheat** `#E0A835` — highlight, sunbursts, "trophy" accents on achievement badges.
- **Charcoal Ink** `#2B2A26` — the true "one-color" ink (a warm near-black, never pure `#000`).
- **Faded Brick** `#8A4B3A` — optional tertiary for regional/desert editions.

**Usage discipline:**
- Any single emblem uses **3–4 of these max**. The whole system stays coherent because everything draws from the same nine.
- Rust Orange + Bureau Green + Canvas Cream is the default "hero" combo. Ember Gold is reserved to signal achievement/trophy (Earn-a-Badge, member editions).
- Regional commemorative badges can shift the *accent* (desert = Faded Brick/Ember Gold; forest = Bureau Green/Dusty Denim) while keeping the same structure and cream base — this is what makes a 40-badge collection feel like a set, not a mess.

### 1.5 Graceful degradation (the make-or-break requirement)
The emblem MUST survive being shrunk to a 2" one-color die-cut sticker and a 2.5" patch. Design the *simplest* version first, then add.

Deliver the emblem in a **four-tier ladder**, each derived from the same master:
1. **Full color / textured** — posters, hero apparel prints, web, packaging. All 3–4 colors + distress.
2. **Flat 3-color** — most stickers, screen-printed tees, embroidered patches. No gradients, no distress; solid spot colors only.
3. **2-color** — cheap stickers, one-side printing, dark-garment prints.
4. **1-color knockout (die-cut master)** — a single-ink silhouette (Charcoal Ink on cream, or reverse/white on dark). This is the version that has to *win*. If the emblem doesn't read at 1.5" in one color, the design is wrong — simplify it before going further.

Rules that guarantee degradation works:
- **Minimum line weight:** nothing thinner than ~1.5pt at 2" reproduction. Topo lines and fine degree rings are the first things that clog on a small patch — build a "simplified" topo with fewer, heavier contours for small sizes.
- **Knockout, don't detail:** at small sizes, convey the scene with *shapes*, not linework. A solid ridgeline silhouette beats an outlined one.
- **Enclosed die-cut silhouette:** the roundel/crest outer edge must be a clean, cuttable shape (no fragile spikes). Die-cutters and embroidery digitizers both need a solid boundary.
- **Text floor:** roundel arc text must stay legible at final size; if it won't, the small-size variant drops the tagline and keeps only `[BRAND]`.
- **Two "official" sizes on the sticker sheet:** a detailed 4"+ version and a simplified ≤2.5" version. Never just scale the big one down.
- **Embroidery reality:** give the digitizer the flat 3-color version and expect to merge tiny details. PVC patches hold finer detail than embroidery — route the intricate designs to PVC, the bold ones to embroidered.

Priority key: **[NOW]** = launch batch, **[LATER]** = phase 2 once the emblem and audience are proven. Prices are suggested retail; many items are seeding giveaways, not margin plays.

## 3. The Killer Product Tie-In: Earn-a-Badge  ⭐ *centerpiece*

This is where merch stops being swag and becomes the growth engine. It fuses the merch line to the app's planned visit-tracking / achievements features and turns "collect all the parks" into a retention loop.

### 3.1 The core mechanic
1. A rider **logs a visit to a park/trail in the app** (check-in via GPS at the location, a scanned trailhead QR, or a manual "I rode here" confirmation with a photo).
2. The app instantly awards a **digital badge** for that specific park — a crest in the heritage system, regionally themed (desert, forest, dune, mountain). It lands in the rider's in-app **Badge Case / Trophy Wall**.
3. The rider can then **claim the physical twin**: a die-cut sticker and/or embroidered-or-PVC patch of that exact park badge. Physical claims are the monetization + seeding surface.
4. Milestones unlock **tiered rewards**: 5 parks, 10, 25, "all parks in [state]," "all X-rated difficulty trails," seasonal challenges. Milestone badges are rarer, shinier (Ember Gold accents), and where numbered/limited editions live.

### 3.2 Physical claim models (pick per phase)
- **Free-with-milestone:** first park badge sticker free (drives the loop); patches unlocked at milestones (5/10/25) either free or discounted. Cost-controlled, maximizes seeding.
- **Pay-to-claim / print-on-demand:** any earned badge can be ordered as a sticker ($3–4) or patch ($8–12), printed on demand and shipped. Turns the collection into a merch catalog that *only unlocks what you've earned* — scarcity by achievement, not by SKU.
- **Quarterly "badge drop" mailer:** batch a rider's newly-earned badges into a periodic envelope to cut shipping cost and create an anticipation moment (the "did my patches come?" dopamine).
- **Operator-fulfilled:** paid operators (the SaaS side) can stock their park's physical badge at the trailhead/office to hand or sell to verified visitors — ties the two-sided model together (see 4.4).

### 3.3 Why it drives BOTH merch demand AND app engagement/retention
- **Merch demand becomes intrinsic, not pushed.** You never have to "sell" a patch — the rider earned it and wants the trophy. Every physical badge is pre-sold by the accomplishment that unlocked it. Demand scales with usage, not with ad spend.
- **Retention via the collection gap.** A half-full Badge Case is a standing reason to open the app and plan the next ride. "Collect all the parks" is an open loop the brain wants to close (the same mechanic behind national-park passport stamps and Pokémon). It converts a one-time discovery tool into a season-long habit.
- **Visit-tracking gets a reason to exist.** Check-ins are usually a feature users ignore. Attaching a collectible reward gives riders a selfish reason to log every visit — which feeds the platform the exact first-party data (real visits, conditions, photos) that makes the discovery product better and the operator analytics valuable.
- **Every physical badge is a new billboard.** The patch goes on a hat, the sticker on the tailgate — each earned badge is another impression seeding the next rider. Engagement literally manufactures marketing.

### 3.4 The "collect all the parks" growth loop
```
Rider logs a park visit in-app
        ↓
Earns digital badge  →  Badge Case now shows the gaps ("12 of 40 parks")
        ↓
Claims physical sticker/patch  →  it goes on the rig / hat
        ↓
Rides more parks to fill the collection  →  MORE app opens, MORE check-ins, MORE first-party data
        ↓
Their covered tailgate + swapped patches are seen by other riders at trailheads/events
        ↓
"What's [BRAND]?" → QR/word-of-mouth → new install → new rider enters the loop
        ↺  (compounds every season)
```
**Reinforcers to build in:**
- **Leaderboards & regional completion:** "You're 3 parks from completing Utah." Publicly shareable completion stamps.
- **Limited seasonal & event badges:** a badge only earnable during a rally weekend or a season → urgency + FOMO, and a reason to travel to new parks.
- **Social share cards:** earning a badge generates a heritage-styled share image (auto-branded, with install link) for Instagram/TikTok — turns the achievement into organic reach.
- **Charter/founder numbering:** early adopters get low member numbers on a founding badge — belonging + status that later users can never get.

**Guardrail:** GPS/QR verification must be real (anti-spoofing) so a badge means something. A trophy that anyone can fake is worthless — and worthless trophies kill the whole loop.

## 4. "Spread the Name" GTM

The mission: convert cheap physical objects into app installs, and let rig decals compound into ambient awareness.

### 4.1 Sticker-seeding tactics
- **Seed, don't sell, at first.** The Trailhead Pack goes out free/near-free with every signup, at every booth, and in every shipped order. A sticker's ROI is measured in years of tailgate impressions, not its $1 cost.
- **Trade into swap culture.** Show up to trailhead/event sticker swaps with the good non-wordmark art (S5) that people *want* to trade for — the brand rides in on aesthetic merit.
- **"Two to share."** Every pack/order includes a couple of extra minis with "give one to a buddy" printing — built-in referral.
- **QR on everything.** Every sticker sheet, koozie, hangtag, and pack sleeve carries a QR → app install / free-first-badge landing page. The object is the ad; the QR is the click.
- **Windshield banners + rig decals as the compounding layer.** Every banner and tailgate strip is a daily, years-long, geographically-mobile impression seen by exactly the right audience (other riders in traffic, at trailheads, in the pits). This is the asset that compounds: 500 banded rigs = a permanent, free, moving ad network in offroad country.

### 4.2 Event / booth presence
- **Target events:** Overland Expo, Off-Road Expo, King of the Hammers, regional jamborees/rallies, and **local swap meets** (highest ROI per dollar — that's where the swap culture and the exact buyers are).
- **Booth as badge station:** don't sell swag — *run the loop live*. "Download, check in here, get your event badge free." A branded backdrop for photos, a bowl of free minis, the seed pack for sale, and a live "wall of earned badges."
- **Event-exclusive commemorative badge:** a dated crest only earnable/available at that event → drives booth traffic and installs on the spot.

### 4.3 Creator / influencer seeding
- **Seed, don't sponsor (at first).** Send free full merch kits (banner, patches, seed pack, hat) to mid-size offroad YouTubers/IG/TikTok creators across all four disciplines. A patch on their pack or a banner on their rig in every video is worth more than a paid post and reads as authentic.
- **Give creators their own park-badge challenge** (e.g., "watch me collect every trail in Colorado") — content that *is* the product demo.
- **Custom creator badge / code** for the few who convert well → move to paid/affiliate on installs.

### 4.4 Ambassador / operator co-branding (the two-sided lever)
- **Parks hand out [BRAND] stickers.** Give paid operators a free co-branded sticker/decal stack ("[BRAND] × [Park Name]") for their trailhead office, gate, or check-in. Riders love a free local sticker; the operator looks generous; the brand seeds at the exact moment of a ride. Near-zero CAC.
- **Operator-stocked park badges** (from 3.2): the park sells/hands its own commemorative badge to verified visitors — operators get a small margin or a loyalty tool, [BRAND] gets seeding + verified check-ins.
- **Rider ambassadors:** power collectors (high badge counts) become ambassadors with a founder patch, referral code, and early access to seasonal badges. Their filled tailgates are the best possible ad.

### 4.5 The funnel: how a $1 sticker becomes an app install
```
$1 sticker seeded (booth / order insert / operator trailhead / buddy hand-off)
        ↓  sticker lands on a rig/helmet/cooler → thousands of passive impressions over years
Another rider sees it → scans the QR (or Googles the name)
        ↓
Lands on "Log your first park, earn your first badge — free" page → installs app
        ↓
Logs first visit → earns first digital badge → claims the free physical badge
        ↓
Now IN the collection loop (Section 3) → rides more, logs more, sticks more
        ↺  their new stickers seed the next rider
```
**Logic:** a sticker is the cheapest, longest-lived, best-targeted ad unit available to this audience. It costs pennies, it's *wanted* (not skipped like a digital ad), it lasts years, and it's already sitting in front of the exact people you want. The QR + free-first-badge offer is what converts the ambient impression into a measurable install. Every install then produces more stickers → the funnel is self-feeding.

**Instrument it:** unique QR/UTM per channel (booth vs. operator vs. order insert vs. creator) so you can see which seeding source actually drives installs and reallocate budget to it.

## 5. Production & Budget

All numbers are **rough industry-ballpark estimates (2026, USD)** for briefing a printer — get live quotes before committing. Ranges depend heavily on quantity, size, and finishing.

### 5.1 Print-on-demand (POD) vs. bulk — the tradeoff
| | Print-on-demand | Bulk / wholesale |
|---|---|---|
| **Upfront cost** | ~$0; pay per order | High; pay for the whole run up front |
| **Per-unit cost** | High (thin margin) | Low (the point of bulk) |
| **Inventory risk** | None | You eat unsold stock |
| **Speed to seed** | Slow, expensive per unit — bad for free seeding | Fast to hand out; cheap per unit |
| **Best for** | Long-tail **park-specific Earn-a-Badge** items (40+ designs, unknown demand each) | High-volume **hero SKUs** you *will* move: seed stickers, core tee, trucker, flagship patch |

**Recommended hybrid:**
- **Bulk** the few high-volume hero SKUs (seed sticker pack, S1/S2 minis, T1/T2, H1 trucker, P1 patch) — you know you'll move them and per-unit cost matters for seeding.
- **POD / on-demand** the long tail of park-specific Earn-a-Badge stickers & patches — you can't pre-guess demand for 40 individual park badges, and on-demand means a new park badge ships day one with zero inventory risk. Several sticker printers and patch makers offer low-/no-MOQ digital printing that functions as POD for die-cut stickers and PVC patches.

### 5.2 Recommended vendor types
- **Die-cut vinyl stickers:** dedicated custom-sticker printers (e.g., StickerMule, Sticker Ninja, StickerApp, or a local sign/vinyl shop for banners). Look for weatherproof/UV vinyl, kiss-cut sheets for packs, low MOQ.
- **PVC & embroidered patches:** custom patch manufacturers (many overseas-sourced via domestic brokers). Ask specifically for **hook-and-loop (Velcro) backing**, and route detailed art to PVC, bold art to embroidered. Woven patches for hat fronts.
- **Windshield/visor banners:** vinyl/sign shops or the sticker printers' banner lines.
- **Apparel & hats (POD):** Printful / Printify / SwiftPOD-style POD for early low-volume tees and embroidered hats; graduate hero tees to a **local screen printer** on tri-blends once volume justifies it (better hand-feel, lower per-unit, the earth-tone garments this audience wants).
- **Enamel pins / koozies:** general promo-product manufacturers (same broker networks as patches).
- **Whip flags:** offroad-specific flag makers — and confirm safety-spec compliance.

### 5.4 Phased budget

**Tier 1 — Scrappy launch (~$3,000–6,000 estimate).** Prove the emblem and the loop.
- Design: 1 emblem system + 4–6 badge variants (freelance/heritage-specialist designer): **~$1,000–2,500** (or less with a fixed-scope package). *This is the highest-leverage dollar — don't cheap out on the master emblem.*
- Stickers: 1,000 minis + 500 3" + 250 banners + 250 seed packs: **~$700–1,200**.
- Patches: 100 PVC + 100 embroidered (incl. mold fee): **~$450–750**.
- Apparel/hats: start **POD** (zero inventory) + a small 48-pc trucker bulk run: **~$400–700**.
- Koozies 150 + a few dozen event minis/props: **~$300–500**.
- Booth basics (banner, table kit) for 1–2 local swap meets: **~$300–600**.
- *Everything else (pins, whips, snapbacks, numbered member editions) waits.*

**Tier 2 — Scaled (~$15,000–35,000 estimate).** Once installs-per-sticker and badge-claim rates are proven.
- Deepen sticker buys to 5k–10k units (per-unit drops to pennies) for aggressive free seeding.
- Bulk screen-print hero tees on tri-blends; expand hat line (snapback, premium patch-front).
- Full Earn-a-Badge POD pipeline stood up for the long tail of park badges (integration + fulfillment).
- Numbered founder patch + pin runs.
- Multi-event circuit (Overland Expo / Off-Road Expo / regional rallies): booth builds, travel, event-exclusive commemorative badge runs.
- Operator co-brand sticker program (free trailhead stacks) across paid parks.
- Creator seeding kits at scale + a few paid/affiliate ambassadors.

**Budget principle:** treat merch spend as **CAC, not COGS**. A $1 sticker that produces even one install over its multi-year life beats most paid digital channels for this audience. Instrument the QR/UTM funnel (4.5) so you can prove it and scale the channels that actually convert.

## 6. Do / Don't Guardrails

### DO
- **DO keep every emblem inside the system:** same crest/roundel/banner architecture, the named palette, ≤2 motifs, and the type pairing. Consistency is what makes 40 badges read as a premium *collection*.
- **DO design the 1-color die-cut master first.** If it doesn't read at 1.5" in one ink, simplify before adding color.
- **DO make it multi-discipline by default.** Two-tracks, topo, and compass read as "offroad" to a dirt-biker, a quad rider, an overlander, and a SxS driver alike. Reserve vehicle-specific silhouettes for opt-in discipline variants — never make the master emblem a single vehicle type.
- **DO lean into "official/heritage" cues** — EST. dates, seals, member numbers, park names, elevations. That's what signals premium and trophy-worthy.
- **DO put a QR + free-first-badge CTA on every seedable object.**
- **DO use real earth tones and sun-faded texture** — the vintage palette is the moat against looking generic.
- **DO verify whip-flag safety specs** and any park-name usage permissions before selling those items.

### DON'T
- **DON'T put OEM logos, trademarks, or model/trim names on product.** No Jeep/Polaris/Can-Am/Honda/Yamaha/KTM/Ford logos, no "Wrangler," "RZR," "Maverick," "Raptor," etc. It's trademark infringement, it's not licensable at this stage, and it instantly reads as bootleg swap-meet junk. Keep the brand's *own* marks the hero.
- **DON'T name or depict a real park/trail on commemorative merch without confirming rights** — for public lands, generic geographic names and your own art are safest; for operator parks, co-brand *with permission* (that's the operator program, not a free-for-all).
- **DON'T drift into generic "Amazon offroad swag":** no chrome/gradient/tribal/skull "beast mode" clichés, no neon, no photo-real 3D badges, no distressed-flag-with-a-4x4 template. The heritage/WPA restraint IS the differentiation.
- **DON'T over-motif.** Three-plus motifs + tread + distress on one small badge turns to mud and cheapens the whole system.
- **DON'T use pure black or pure white as "ink."** Warm Charcoal `#2B2A26` and Canvas Cream `#F2E8CF` keep even the 1-color pieces looking vintage, not clip-art.
- **DON'T sell a badge that can be faked.** Earn-a-Badge only works if check-ins are verified — a meaningless trophy kills the collection loop.
- **DON'T treat merch as a profit center early.** If margin obsession stops you from seeding stickers freely, you've defeated the entire acquisition strategy. Optimize for impressions and installs first; margin comes later at scale.
